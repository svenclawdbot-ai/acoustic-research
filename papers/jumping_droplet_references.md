# Jumping Droplet Condensation - Key References

## The Seminal Paper (Start Here)

### Boreyko & Chen 2009 - THE Discovery Paper
**Title:** Self-propelled dropwise condensate on superhydrophobic surfaces  
**Authors:** Jonathan B. Boreyko, Chuan-Hua Chen  
**Journal:** Physical Review Letters  
**Year:** 2009  
**Volume:** 103, Issue 18  
**Pages:** 184501  
**DOI:** 10.1103/PhysRevLett.103.184501  

**Download:**
- arXiv: https://arxiv.org/abs/0910.3602 (FREE PREPRINT)
- Physical Review: https://journals.aps.org/prl/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevLett.103.184501

**Key Finding:** First demonstration that condensing droplets can spontaneously jump off superhydrophobic surfaces without gravity or external forces. The coalescence releases surface energy that converts to kinetic energy, ejecting droplets at ~1 m/s.

**Why It Matters:** This discovery enabled 3-10× heat transfer enhancement by eliminating the insulating liquid film.

## Key Equations (From Boreyko & Chen 2009)

### Jumping Velocity
```
v = √(2 × ΔE_surface / m)

Where:
- ΔE_surface = σ × (A_initial - A_final) [Surface energy released]
- m = ρ × (4/3)πr³ [Droplet mass]
- σ = surface tension (N/m)
- r = droplet radius (m)
```

### For Two Equal Droplets (radius r):
```
v ≈ √(0.82 × σ / (ρ × r))

Typical values:
- Water: σ = 0.072 N/m, ρ = 1000 kg/m³
- r = 10 μm → v ≈ 0.8 m/s
- r = 50 μm → v ≈ 0.35 m/s
```

### Optimal Droplet Size:
- **10-100 μm** radius: Best for jumping
- **< 10 μm:** Too small, not enough energy
- **> 100 μm:** Too heavy, gravity dominates
