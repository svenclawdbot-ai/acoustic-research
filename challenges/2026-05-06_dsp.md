# Wednesday Engineering Challenge — Digital Signal Processing
## Date: May 6, 2026 | Topic: Quadrature Sampling & Digital Beamforming

### The Problem

You're designing the TurboQuant V5 FPGA acquisition pipeline. The Red Pitaya STEMlab 125-14 samples the received echo at 125 MSPS with a 14-bit ADC. Your transducer center frequency is 3.5 MHz. You need to implement **quadrature sampling** (I/Q demodulation) digitally on the FPGA to reduce data rate and enable real-time beamforming.

### Part A: Theoretical Foundation

1. **Nyquist zone selection:** The RF echo is band-limited to 2.5–4.5 MHz. Which Nyquist zone does this sit in? What is the minimum sampling rate required by the Nyquist theorem? Why can we sample at 125 MSPS and still recover the signal?

2. **Digital downconversion:** Write the mathematical expression for mixing the sampled signal x[n] with a digital local oscillator at 3.5 MHz. Show that this produces baseband I/Q components plus an image at 7 MHz. How do you suppress the image?

3. **Decimation & anti-aliasing:** After mixing, you want to decimate by factor M to reduce the data rate. The baseband signal has bandwidth ~1 MHz (2.5–4.5 MHz shifted to -1 to +1 MHz). What is the maximum decimation factor M before aliasing? Design a 4-tap FIR filter (symmetric, linear phase) with coefficients h[0..3] that provides >40 dB attenuation at the aliasing frequency.

### Part B: FPGA Implementation

You're using the Red Pitaya's Xilinx Zynq-7010 FPGA. The ADC provides 14-bit samples at 125 MSPS. You have a 125 MHz system clock.

4. **Resource estimation:** The digital mixer uses a DDS (Direct Digital Synthesizer) with a 32-bit phase accumulator. Calculate:
   - The phase increment word for 3.5 MHz output
   - The frequency resolution of the DDS
   - The LUT cost for a sin/cos lookup table (1/4 symmetry, 12-bit amplitude)

5. **Fixed-point arithmetic:** The mixer multiplies 14-bit ADC samples by 12-bit DDS outputs, producing 26-bit products. You need to round to 16-bit for the FIR filter input. Where should you place the binary point? What is the quantization noise power relative to the signal? Use the formula: SQNR = 6.02N + 1.76 dB, where N = effective bits.

6. **Pipeline timing:** The FIR filter is 32 taps, running at 125 MHz. After decimation by M=10, the output rate is 12.5 MHz. Draw a timing diagram showing:
   - ADC samples arriving at 125 MHz
   - Mixer producing I/Q at 125 MHz
   - FIR producing one output every M=10 cycles
   - The required FIFO depth to buffer outputs for the DMA engine

### Part C: Real-World Constraint

7. **Dynamic range budget:** The received echo amplitude varies from:
   - Large reflection (specular): ~500 mV peak at ADC input
   - Speckle noise floor: ~0.5 mV (determined by thermal noise in 50Ω at 300K over 2 MHz BW)
   
   Calculate:
   - The required ADC dynamic range in dB
   - Whether 14 bits is sufficient (theoretical maximum SNR)
   - The maximum tolerable FPGA mixer spurious-free dynamic range (SFDR) to avoid masking the noise floor

8. **Phase-coherent beamforming:** For a linear array with 8 elements spaced at λ/2 (where λ = c/f₀, c=1540 m/s, f₀=3.5 MHz), calculate the inter-element delay for a beam steered to θ = 30° off-axis. Express this as a sample delay at the decimated rate (12.5 MHz). Is this an integer number of samples? If not, how do you implement fractional delay in FPGA (hint: polyphase filter)?

### Deliverables

- Hand calculations for Parts A and C (show your work)
- Verilog/VHDL pseudocode or detailed block diagram for Part B
- A 1-page summary of your FPGA architecture with resource estimates
- **Bonus:** Simulate your FIR filter in Python (scipy.signal.firwin) and plot the frequency response. Verify the -40 dB point.

3–4 hours of focused work. This is not a trivia quiz — it's a real design problem you'll face when implementing the TurboQuant FPGA firmware.
