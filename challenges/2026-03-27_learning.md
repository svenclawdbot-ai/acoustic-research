### Part 1: The Accidental Discovery (45 min)

On November 8, 1895, Wilhelm Röntgen noticed a fluorescent screen glowing across the room while running a cathode ray tube. Within weeks, he had imaged his wife Anna Bertha's hand — the first medical X-ray.

**Research & reflect:**
1. How did the medical establishment react to X-rays in the first 5 years? Was adoption fast or slow? What drove resistance?
2. The early pioneers suffered horribly — radiation burns, amputations, cancers. At what point did the medical community acknowledge the danger, and how did regulation emerge?
3. Compare the X-ray timeline to AI in medicine today: does the pattern of "excitement → harm → regulation → mature adoption" repeat?

**Source suggestions:** Look for Röntgen's original 1895 paper "On a New Kind of Rays" (*Über eine neue Art von Strahlen*). The Radiology Centennial publications (1995) are also rich.

### Part 2: Sound as Sight — The Ultrasound Revolution (60 min)

Ultrasound imaging has a tangled origin: SONAR in WWI/WWII, industrial flaw detection in the 1940s, and then the pivotal work of Ian Donald in Glasgow (1958) who brought it into obstetrics.

**Research & reflect:**
1. Ian Donald was a gynaecologist who borrowed industrial flaw-detection equipment from Babcock & Wilcox (a boilermaker). What does this cross-pollination between industrial NDE and medical imaging tell you about innovation?
2. The path from industrial ultrasound → medical ultrasound → elastography mirrors your own research trajectory. Map the key transitions: who were the bridge figures?
3. Elastography itself was proposed by Ophir et al. (1991) — only 35 years ago. Why did it take so long after B-mode ultrasound (1960s) for someone to think of measuring tissue stiffness with sound?

**Key figures to investigate:** Ian Donald, John Wild, Douglass Howry, Jonathan Ophir, Armen Sarvazyan

### Part 3: The Democratisation Question (45 min)

Medical imaging technology follows a pattern: expensive → specialist → portable → ubiquitous. MRI machines cost millions; handheld ultrasound probes now cost hundreds.

**Research & reflect:**
1. The Butterfly iQ (handheld ultrasound, ~$2,000) was called "the stethoscope of the 21st century." What happened to diagnostic accuracy when imaging moved from specialist radiologists to general practitioners?
2. Your ESP32-S3 array controller costs under £50 in components. If shear wave elastography could be done with a £200 handheld device, what would change in global healthcare? Think specifically about:
   - Liver fibrosis screening in sub-Saharan Africa (where hepatitis B prevalence is 6-8%)
   - Breast cancer screening in communities without mammography
   - Point-of-care assessment in conflict zones
3. Is there a moral obligation to make medical imaging affordable, or is the market sufficient? Engage with at least one ethical framework (utilitarian, Rawlsian, capabilities approach).
