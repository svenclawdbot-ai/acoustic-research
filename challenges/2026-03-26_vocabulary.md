# Vocabulary Deep Dive — Wave Physics & Elastography
## March 26, 2026 — Thursday Language Challenge

---

### 1. **Elastography** /ɪˌlæsˈtɒɡrəfi/

**Etymology:** Greek *elastos* (ἐλαστός) "ductile, flexible" + *-graphy* (γραφία) "writing, recording"
- *Elastos* from *elaunein* (ἐλαύνειν) "to drive, beat out" — originally described beaten metal
- *Graphy* from *graphein* (γράφειν) "to write"

**Definition:** Medical imaging modality that maps the elastic properties (stiffness) of soft tissue by tracking shear wave propagation or measuring tissue displacement under stress.

**Context:** "Magnetic resonance elastography (MRE) can detect liver fibrosis stages non-invasively by measuring tissue stiffness."

---

### 2. **Dispersion** /dɪˈspɜːʃən/

**Etymology:** Latin *dispersio* from *dispergere* "to scatter in different directions"
- *Dis-* (apart, in different directions) + *spargere* (to scatter, strew)
- Related to English "sparse," "spark"

**Definition:** The phenomenon where wave phase velocity varies with frequency, causing different frequency components to travel at different speeds and leading to waveform distortion over distance.

**Context:** "In viscoelastic materials, shear wave dispersion means high-frequency components arrive before low-frequency components, complicating arrival-time measurements."

---

### 3. **Attenuation** /əˌtenjuˈeɪʃən/

**Etymology:** Latin *attenuare* from *ad-* (to, toward) + *tenuis* (thin, fine, weak)
- *Tenuis* shares root with "tenuous," "extenuate," "tenuity"
- Original sense: to make thin or weak

**Definition:** The gradual loss of wave energy (amplitude decrease) as it propagates through a medium, typically due to absorption, scattering, or geometric spreading.

**Context:** "Soft tissue attenuation of ultrasound follows approximately 0.5 dB/cm/MHz, forcing trade-offs between penetration depth and resolution."

---

### 4. **Viscoelastic** /ˌvɪskəʊɪˈlæstɪk/

**Etymology:** Hybrid compound — Latin *viscum* (birdlime, sticky substance) + Greek *elastos* (flexible)
- *Viscous* from *viscum* — the sticky sap of mistletoe used as adhesive
- Combines fluid-like (viscous) and solid-like (elastic) behavior

**Definition:** Material behavior exhibiting both viscous (time-dependent, energy-dissipating) and elastic (instantaneous, energy-storing) characteristics when deformed.

**Context:** "Biological tissues are viscoelastic: they store energy like springs but also dissipate it like dashpots, exhibiting frequency-dependent stiffness."

---

### 5. **Modulus** /ˈmɒdjʊləs/

**Etymology:** Latin *modulus*, diminutive of *modus* (measure, manner, way)
- *Modus* gives us "mode," "model," "moderate," "modulate"
- Diminutive sense: "a small measure" or "unit of measurement"

**Definition:** A quantitative measure of a material's resistance to deformation; specifically, the ratio of stress to strain in the linear elastic regime.

**Context:** "The shear modulus G quantifies resistance to shear deformation, while Young's modulus E describes resistance to axial stretching."

---

### 6. **Shear** /ʃɪə/

**Etymology:** Old English *sceran* (to cut, cleave) — Germanic origin
- Cognate with German *scheren*, Dutch *scheren* (to shear, cut)
- Originally referred to cutting wool from sheep; evolved to mean sliding deformation

**Definition:** A deformation mode where parallel planes in a material slide past each other, perpendicular to the applied force; characterized by strain without volume change.

**Context:** "Shear waves (S-waves) cannot travel through fluids because fluids have zero shear modulus — they cannot support transverse particle motion."

---

### 7. **Evanescent** /ˈɛvənɛsənt/

**Etymology:** Latin *evanescere* from *e-* (out, away) + *vanescere* (to vanish)
- *Vanus* (empty) gives us "vanity," "vanish," "evanesce"
- Sense: "tending to vanish like vapor"

**Definition:** Describes waves that decay exponentially in amplitude with distance, rather than propagating indefinitely; occurs in total internal reflection and near-field acoustic/optical phenomena.

**Context:** "Evanescent waves in the near-field of an ultrasound transducer carry high-resolution information but decay within a wavelength of the source."

---

### 8. **Anisotropy** /ˌænaɪˈsɒtrəpi/

**Etymology:** Greek *an-* (ἀν-, not, without) + *isos* (ἴσος, equal) + *tropos* (τρόπος, direction, way)
- *Isos* gives us "isometric," "isotope," "isobar"
- *Tropos* gives us "tropic," "trophy," "tropical"

**Definition:** The property of a material having directionally dependent physical properties; different values when measured along different axes.

**Context:** "Skeletal muscle exhibits anisotropy: shear waves travel faster along muscle fibers than across them, complicating stiffness quantification."

---

### 9. **Phase velocity** /feɪz vəˈlɒsɪti/

**Etymology:** *Phase* from Greek *phasis* (φάσις) "appearance, phase of the moon" — from *phainein* (to show, appear)
- *Velocity* from Latin *velocitas* from *velox* (swift)

**Definition:** The speed at which a specific phase of a wave (e.g., a crest or zero-crossing) propagates through space; given by $c_p = \omega/k$ where $\omega$ is angular frequency and $k$ is wavenumber.

**Context:** "In dispersive media, phase velocity varies with frequency, causing wave packets to change shape as they propagate."

---

### 10. **Tomography** /təˈmɒɡrəfi/

**Etymology:** Greek *tomos* (τόμος) "slice, section" + *graphein* (γράφειν) "to write"
- *Temos* from *temnein* (τέμνειν) "to cut" — gives us "atom" (uncuttable), "epitome," "anatomy"
- Original imaging technique literally reconstructed "slices" of the body

**Definition:** Imaging technique that reconstructs cross-sectional (slice) images of an object from multiple projections or measurements around its periphery.

**Context:** "Ultrasound tomography reconstructs sound speed and attenuation maps from transmission measurements, revealing tissue microstructure."
